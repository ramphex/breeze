## Design Context

### Users
IT technicians managing fleets of endpoints (primary), IT managers reviewing dashboards (secondary). Techs are often multi-tasking across monitors, triaging alerts, running scripts, remoting into machines. They need information density without overwhelm. Managers need quick status reads.

### Brand Personality
**Fast, reliable, approachable.** Breeze should feel like a calm control center — not frantic, not sterile. Confidence-inspiring without being corporate or cold. The name "Breeze" implies effortlessness.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Tone**: Business/professional with warmth. Think: competent colleague, not enterprise software.
- **Reference**: NinjaOne — clean, functional, professional RMM UI. Take inspiration from the clarity and information hierarchy, not the specific visual language.
- **Anti-reference**: AI vibe-coded aesthetics — gradient text, glassmorphism, neon-on-dark, identical card grids, generic system fonts with no personality. The current UI acknowledges this is the starting point to move away from.
- **Theme**: Light mode primary, dark mode supported. Both should feel intentional.
- **Typography**: Open to evolving beyond system defaults. Should feel professional but not generic.
- **Color**: Open to evolving beyond default blue. Should support status semantics (green/red/orange/yellow) clearly.

### Design Principles
1. **Calm control** — Reduce cognitive load. Surface what matters, hide what doesn't. No visual noise.
2. **Information-first** — Data density over decoration. Every pixel should earn its place.
3. **Instantly scannable** — Techs glance, they don't read. Visual hierarchy must be ruthless.
4. **Professionally warm** — Not cold enterprise, not playful startup. Competent and approachable.
5. **No AI tells** — Every design choice should look like a human designer made it deliberately.
